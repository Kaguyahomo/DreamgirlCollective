I Saw Her Standing There

Catchy! To call this song youthful would be an understatement. What Lennon and McCartney call love I’d wager is just in their ragers. The hand claps as percussion goes a long way in creating the atmosphere of this being performed at a live event, where the scene of the song would occur. Likewise since this first album of theirs is all the catalog they had from doing live shows, it was probably a scene the band had seen many times. The song certainly isn’t sonically complex, but leading off their debut with an original instead of the covers is pretty savvy!

Misery

The piano section in this is beautiful, if not remarkarable. Sticks to its youthful guns, saying nothing particular but universally applicable. The juxtaposition of the content and the chords doesn't work as well after a couple of listens. Lennon and McCartney are credited as the songwriters, though I'm inferring the tone of the lyrics is leaning towards Lennon. He was always supposed to be the more 'ironic' writer.

Anna (Go to Him)

The first cover! Consistent to the albums theme! It's in line with my preferences, but Lennon's scatchy vocals are gorgeous. You can hear the strain and exhaustian in such an endearing fashion.

Chains

Love Carole King as a song writer. Not the biggest fan of Harrison's vocals on this track. Who doesn't love a fade-out?

Boys

Okay but who know Ringo could do such a good blues impression? The yowl on this track is charming. No wonder they were such heartthrobs.

Ask Me Why

After a run of covers were back to the dynamic duo of Mccartney and Lennon. Though this song doesn't have quite the pack to punch it certainly has some highlights. This song has the BEST fadeout on the album. I'm definitley displaying a bias towards Lennon's vocals. They're just so heavenly. Can't get as much play as a single because of its mellower demeanor but I prefer it to 'Misery'. To which this song is definitley sisters, as it's delivery of the line "I can't conceive of any more misery". Given this album's role is to introduce the raw potential of the quintent, this is a high-point of the groups budding lyrical prowess.

Please Please Me

Titular! Lennon and his harmonica I know Bushwick would spit you out with some excellent scratchy tattoos. The picking on the electric guitar is pretty impressive, really balances out the surfrock aesthetic. McCartney and Lennon are out in full force with their duets here especially the raspy "Come on's" of Lennon Ringo showing out on the drums here as well. The only thing this song is lacking is complexity! Sonically and lyrically this leaves no imprint.

Love Me Do

Poor Ringo on the tambourine. The Front Bottoms did your contribution to this song justice and I hope you got to hear it. We get it John you own a harmonica. McCartney can hold his own vocally.

P.S. I Love You

I hope one day it becomes the common take that Ringo was the most important Beatle. I'd like to see John's egotistical ass pick up a fucking Maraca. The highest gear the band has vocally at this stage in their discography is when McCartney is singing somber and sultry. Lenon's backup vocals on this song are mixed terribly! So jarring to just have him come in so strong on single words. This song owes in particular owes so much to the Blues. McCartney in particular I imagine doing the elvis pose when he sings "You know I want you to" At this point in the album I am sick of hearing the word love. I dont think they knew how to end this song.

Baby It's You

Was a good time to do a cover, the band's inexperience as songwriters was starting to become apparent. Harrison and McCartney's background harmonys on this joint are divine. It's of legend at this point that this album was recorded in a day, but it really comes through that Lennon is pulling his voice for the finale.

Do You Want to Know a Secret

Harrison isn't bad on this track but he really struggles to hold a note. There are some times he definitley comes up flat as well. The mixing on McCartney and Lennon's backup harmonies makes them sound so ghostly. The strumming intro to this song is absolutley a highpoint of the album soncially. For an album that at times is really cookie cutter, and this song especially, those sonic moments were true flashes of brilliance. For being an original, this is probably there most daring attempt at lyrics for a love song on the album.

A Taste of Honey

While I generally prefer the Beatle's slower moments, this song doesn't do it for me. It almost sounds like a folkly Zeppelin song in a way. Harrison and a patchy Lennon really stuggle to keep up with McCartney on this track.

There's a Place

Lennon harmonica drinking game? Lennon can really go high on those duets with McCartney! Pretty impressive. More sappy and un-inspired original lyrics.

Twist and Shout

This song is immortalized as a cover so excellent it becomes the reference for the song, surpassing the original. Lennon sounds like his larynx is against the edge of wire and it's so sexy. High point of this song is absolutely the four-part harmony they pull off twice! That's how you end an album! The instrumentation is so celbratory and final. Enough can't be said about Lennon's vocals on this tracl. Rolled across gravel and the distance of the bar to ask for a dance. Their little shouts are just captivating. They were having so much fun!
